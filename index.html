Research

Who are we
designing for?

Three distinct user groups each with different contexts but the same core frustration: grocery shopping feels chaotic and wasteful.

TM

Tumukunde, 22

University student · Makerere

"I always forget something and have to go back, such a waste of time and money."

  • Forgets items mid shop
  • Overspends without realising
  • No budget tracking system
  • Stick to a weekly budget
  • Shop in one trip
KS

Kanyesigye, 34

Marketing manager · Bukoto

"I buy the same things twice because I can't remember what I already have."

  • Buys duplicates → food waste
  • List on paper, always lost
  • No time to plan properly
  • Reduce food waste
  • Quick organised trips
NE

Natembo, 41

Parent · Ntinda

"I send my husband to the store and he still comes back with the wrong thing."

  • Can't share list with family
  • Loses track of total spend
  • Items bought wrong or missed
  • Know total before checkout
  • Export list to share
Define

"People across all life stages struggle to shop efficiently because they forget items, overspend unknowingly, and have no organised system to plan and track their groceries."

HMW 01

How might we help users never forget an item by making list creation fast and effortless?

HMW 02

How might we help users stay within budget by showing their estimated spend before checkout?

HMW 03

How might we help users track what's in the trolley vs what still needs to be picked up?

HMW 04

How might we make category grouping so natural that shopping in store feels logical and fast?

Reflect

What I
learned

The most valuable part of any project isn't what you built — it's what you'd do differently next time.

01

Research before pixels

Building personas and journey maps first meant every design decision had a reason. "Why does this exist?" was always answerable.

02

Design systems save time

Defining the navy palette, glassmorphism cards and spacing upfront meant no inconsistency across screens.

03

Empty states matter

The first thing a new user sees is an empty list. That moment needed its own screen — it's the first impression of the whole app.

04

Budget feature = real gap

No competitor has a built-in budget tracker with live item pricing. Identifying that gap through competitor analysis made the app genuinely different.

05

Security is UX too

Password hashing, JWT tokens, and validation messages aren't just backend work — they're part of the user's trust in the product.

06

Next: real user testing

The next iteration would involve testing with real users — watching 5 people use it live reveals more than a month of solo design.
